History of climate change science

The history of the scientific discovery of climate change began in the early 19th century when ice ages and other natural changes in paleoclimate were first suspected and the natural greenhouse effect was first identified. In the late 19th century, scientists first argued that human emissions of greenhouse gases could change Earth's energy balance and climate. Many other theories of climate change were advanced, involving forces from volcanism to solar variation. In the 1960s, the evidence for the warming effect of carbon dioxide gas became increasingly convincing. Some scientists also pointed out that human activities that generated atmospheric aerosols (e.g., "pollution") could have cooling effects as well.

During the 1970s, scientific opinion increasingly favored the warming viewpoint. By the 1990s, as the result of improving the fidelity of computer models and observational work confirming the Milankovitch theory of the ice ages, a consensus position formed: greenhouse gases were deeply involved in most climate changes and human-caused emissions were bringing discernible global warming. Since the 1990s, scientific research on climate change has included multiple disciplines and has expanded. Research has expanded our understanding of causal relations, links with historic data, and abilities to measure and model climate change. Research during this period has been summarized in the Assessment Reports by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change.

Climate change, broadly interpreted, is a significant and lasting change in the statistical distribution of weather patterns over periods ranging from decades to millions of years. It may be a change in average weather conditions or in the distribution of weather around the average conditions (such as more or fewer extreme weather events). Climate change is caused by factors that include oceanic processes (such as oceanic circulation), biotic processes (e.g., plants), variations in solar radiation received by Earth, plate tectonics and volcanic eruptions, and human-induced alterations of the natural world. This last effect is currently causing global warming, and "climate change" is often used to describe human-specific impacts.

Climate Change discoveries


1938 - Proof that global temperatures are rising


Steam engineer Callendar made the decision to take a break from his regular work in 1938 and started methodically compiling data from 147 weather stations throughout the world. He conducted all of his calculations by hand and found that, over the previous 50 years, the average world temperature had increased by 0.3°C.


1954 - The birth of the solar cell


Bell Labs, the research division of the Bell Telephone Company in New Jersey, requested assistance from engineer Daryl Chapin in 1952 to address a challenge. The business sought to expand its telephone network into isolated tropical areas. The conventional batteries used at the time weren't adequate, though, as they deteriorated too quickly in the hot, muggy weather. When Chapin was requested to investigate other power sources, he thought solar power might be an option.


1958 - CO2 levels are rising, and fossil fuels are to blame


Dr Charles David Keeling provides the first evidence that CO2 levels are rising In 1958, a young postgraduate geochemist called Charles David Keeling decided to compare the amounts of CO2in water and air. Nobody had ever really tried to measure the level of CO2 in the atmosphere before, so there was no off-the-shelf equipment he could use. He designed his own apparatus and set off to a weather observation station on the top of the Mauna Loa volcano in Hawaii. Once there, he took meticulous measurements every day and within five years he had provided the first unequivocal proof that CO2 concentrations were rising. What’s more, by analysing the CO2 in his samples, Dr Keeling was able to attribute this rise to the use of fossil fuels.


1967 - Earth’s changing climate modelled for first time


The first climate computer model of the planet Earth is produced by scientists. According to the model, doubling CO2 concentrations might result in a 2°C increase in global temperatures. Researchers Richard Wetherald and Syukuro Manabe created the first precise computer model of Earth's climate in 1967. The atmosphere, seas, clouds, and their interactions with one another were all considered as part of the model's analysis of the various climate-related factors.


1968 - Scientist predicts melting ice caps


A catastrophic rise in sea levels could result from the loss of the Antarctic ice sheets, according to Dr. John Mercer, a glaciologist at Ohio State University in Columbus. Dr. Mercer found signs of a former freshwater lake while conducting fieldwork at the Reedy Glacier in West Antarctica in 1968. The lake's remnants were located 1,400 meters above sea level in the Transantarctic Mountains. Dr. Mercer saw this as proof that the West Antarctic Ice Sheet as a whole once melted away, which was previously thought to be impossible.


1969 - Earth’s temperature measured with satellites for first time


Launch of NASA's Nimbus III satellite into orbit, which delivers the first precise measurement of air temperatures on a worldwide scale. How scientists investigate the Earth's climate, weather patterns, and atmosphere has been transformed by the Nimbus satellites. The series of satellites, which were first launched in 1964, gave us access to previously unobtainable information on global temperatures, the amount of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, the ozone layer, and the thickness of sea ice over the course of 30 years. Additionally, it made it feasible for scientists to create computer models that could predict the weather up to two weeks in advance, something that was previously almost impossible.


1985 - Drilling 150,000 years deeper into the ice


The previous 150,000 years have seen an ups and downs in CO2 and temperature, according to ice cores from Antarctica. Ice cores provide key information about what the Earth’s climate was like in the past. A special snapshot of the environment at the moment can be found in bubbles sealed in the ice. By examining the proportion of "heavy" oxygen and hydrogen atoms, scientists can also determine historical surface temperatures.


1985 - Ozone hole discovery


Scientists discover a hole in the ozone layer above Antarctica. We are naturally shielded from harmful ultraviolet (UV) light by the ozone layer in the Earth's atmosphere. Therefore, the world was astonished to learn in 1985 that three BAS scientists had discovered very low ozone levels above the South Pole.


1985 - The lithium-ion battery is invented


Professor Akira Yoshino, a physicist from Japan, creates the initial rechargeable lithium battery. The compact battery starts a revolution in energy storage that eventually powers portable electronics like laptops and mobile phones.


1988 - The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) heralds a new era of climate research


The IPCC regularly provides policymakers with scientific assessments on the level of our understanding of climate change. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) is the UN agency tasked with evaluating the science relating to climate change. It was established by the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) and the World Meteorological Organization (WMO). 


1991 - Wind power takes off in the UK


The first wind farm in the UK is being built in Cornwall, and it consists of 10 turbines that combined can power about 2,700 households. The Edwards family finished and launched the UK's first commercial wind farm, Delabole Wind Farm, in North Cornwall in 1991.


1992 - Coral reefs at threa


Scientists are aware that corals and other creatures will find it more difficult to form reefs as ocean CO2 levels rise. The ocean's water becomes more acidic as a result of CO2 dissolving there. This prevents corals from ingesting calcium carbonate, a necessary mineral that they need to form their skeletons.


1994 - First climate change legislation comes into force


197 nations ratify the first global agreement to fight climate change. The first worldwide agreement to regulate greenhouse gas emissions and stop climate change was the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). 197 nations have ratified it, and it came into effect on March 21st, 1994.


1996 - Capturing carbon underground


The first carbon storage project in history begins. When greenhouse gases produced by factories, power plants, and other sources are taken and stored underground, this process is known as carbon capture (CC). As a result, there is no longer any CO2 emitted into the atmosphere, halting future global warming.


2003 - Scientists link extreme weather to climate change


A heatwave in Europe kills tens of thousands of people – scientists say that climate change is to blame.


2007 - The Arctic is warming twice as fast as the rest of the planet


arctic bears and other species are at risk because the arctic areas are warming up twice as quickly as the rest of the planet. The greatest investigation of the polar areas of Earth was carried out during the International Polar Year 2007–2008. In order to discover the mysteries of the Arctic and Antarctic, approximately 50,000 scientists, students, and support personnel from over 60 countries gathered. What they discovered was disturbing. Parts of the Antarctic ice sheet, the Greenland ice sheet, and the Arctic sea ice are all melting at rates that have never been seen in the previous 10,000 years.


2008 - The UK Climate Change Act is enshrined in law


The Act becomes the world’s first long-term legally binding framework for tackling climate change The Act requires the government to set binding five-year carbon budgets based on the latest science.


2015 - Landmark international Paris agreement reached to cut carbon


The Paris Agreement is ratified by 196 nations, which commits them to keeping global warming to less than 2°C over pre-industrial levels. An international agreement on climate change that is legally binding is the Paris Agreement. It was approved by 196 nations on December 12th, 2015, and went into effect on November 4th, 2016. The target is to keep warming to 1.5°C below pre-industrial levels.


2019 - Ice collapse ‘irreversible’


Greenland's and Antarctica's ice sheets may already be past the point of no return, and their collapse might result in a 10 meter rise in sea level. According to a special assessment by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), the Amundsen Sea embayment of West Antarctica, a portion of an ice sheet, may already have reached a tipping point and collapse is now certain.


2019 - UK first country to pass net zero emissions law


The UK is the first developed nation to adopt regulations requiring large economies to reduce their contributions to global warming by 2050. The UK pledged in 2019 to achieve net zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050. When the quantity of greenhouse gas emissions created and those taken out of the atmosphere are equal, the situation is known as net zero.


2019 - Variety of life on Earth being lost at “unprecedented” pace


Currently, up to one million animal and plant species are in danger of going extinct, and many of them might do so over the next few decades. The number of native species on the earth has drastically decreased since 1900, according to a 2019 UN report. The study is the most thorough analysis of world biodiversity that has ever been done, and it was prepared by 145 specialists from 50 different nations.


2020 - World record set for solar power


With a cell that can convert 29.52% of solar energy into electricity, Oxford PV, an Oxford University spinoff firm, establishes a global record.


2021 - Many aspects of climate change are now inevitable and irreversible


By 2040, the earth will have warmed by 1.5C, according to scientists, defying past predictions. At that rate of warming, there will be an increase in heatwaves, severe storms, droughts, and flooding. The results are detailed in the IPCC's most recent Assessment Report, which is the premier source of information on climate science worldwide. The study issues the experts' most stern warning to date on the growing climate disaster. It issues a warning that we are already witnessing climatic shifts that have not been seen in thousands, if not hundreds of thousands of years.
